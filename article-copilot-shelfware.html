AI & Copilot

The Copilot sprawl pattern nobody is talking about: shelfware at scale.

From 18 client deployments, fewer than 1 in 3 Copilot seats see weekly active use after month four. Here's the deployment governance pattern that flips that ratio — and the contracts language that makes it possible.

VP
Vinod Patel
Managing Partner, Proteam Advisory
· 21 February 2026 · 7 min read

The Copilot rollout pattern is becoming familiar. A senior leader sees a demo, the procurement signature happens fast, and 2,000 seats land in the tenant. By month four, the dashboards show that fewer than 600 of those seats are seeing weekly active use. By month six, a CFO is asking why the line item didn't deliver.

Across 18 client deployments we've reviewed, the pattern holds. Average month-four weekly active rate: 31%. Best in class: 68%. Worst: 11%. The variance isn't random — it tracks against deployment governance, not employee enthusiasm.

The shelfware mechanics

Microsoft 365 Copilot is sold as an add-on subscription, typically $30 per user per month, on top of an underlying Microsoft 365 licence. For an enterprise of 5,000 seats, the gross commitment runs to $1.8M annually. That figure is before any consumption variance — Copilot doesn't bill differently for active versus dormant users.

The shelfware pattern develops in three predictable stages.

Stage 1: The pilot honeymoon

Pilot users self-select. They're enthusiastic, technical, and willing to learn new prompt patterns. Pilot adoption rates routinely exceed 70%. This produces the business case for the broader rollout — which is then assumed to scale linearly.

Stage 2: The general rollout

Seats are assigned to a much broader population. Many of these users haven't asked for Copilot, don't have a clear use case in mind, and don't know how to prompt effectively. The first two weeks see novelty-driven exploration. Weeks three to eight see drop-off as the novelty fades and the user reverts to old workflows.

Stage 3: The plateau

By month four, weekly active rate has settled. The users who are using it are using it well. The users who aren't have stopped trying. Without intervention, the plateau stays where it lands.

The Copilot question isn't whether your people will adopt AI. It's whether the deployment governance puts the licence in front of the right user, with the right prompt patterns, at the moment the work happens.

The deployment governance pattern that works

The clients with the highest sustained adoption rates share four practices.

1. Persona-led rollout, not org-chart rollout

Rather than allocating seats by department or seniority, the high-performing deployments map seats to "Copilot persona" — knowledge workers whose daily output is high-volume document creation, analysis, or correspondence. Engineering managers, sales operations analysts, finance business partners, paralegals, and bid managers are characteristic high-fit personas. Software engineers (who tend to use GitHub Copilot or Cursor instead) and customer support agents (whose workflows are too templated) are characteristically low-fit.

2. Prompt-pattern training, not feature training

Most "Copilot training" we've seen walks users through where the buttons are. The training that drives sustained usage walks users through five to ten prompt patterns specific to their job — "draft a status update from this Teams thread", "summarise this 47-page bid response in three paragraphs", "build a slide outline from this meeting recording". The patterns matter more than the features.

3. Quarterly active-use audits with reclaim

Microsoft's admin centre exposes weekly active use data per user. The deployments that hold their adoption rate run a quarterly review: any user with zero weekly active sessions in the previous 12 weeks has the seat reclaimed and put back in the pool. Reassignment is positioned as a feature ("we'll get this back to a colleague who'll use it") not a punishment. The reclaim rate after the first quarter is usually 15-25% of total seats.

4. Renewal mechanics that mirror the reclaim

Microsoft's standard Copilot subscription is annual. The reclaim cycle should map to that — either via a Microsoft Customer Agreement with monthly true-down (now available for many enterprise customers) or via a structured under-commit at renewal with the option to add seats mid-term. Without one of those mechanisms, the reclaim is operationally clean but financially toothless until the next anniversary.

The contract clauses that change the conversation

Three contract levers are worth pushing on at the next renewal.

Annual true-down rights. Negotiate the right to reduce committed seat count by up to 10-15% at the anniversary, without penalty. Microsoft will resist this; persistence helps.

Co-term with the underlying M365 EA. Aligning the Copilot commitment with the broader EA renewal gives more leverage on both contracts. A standalone Copilot SKU evaluated in isolation is a weaker negotiation position.

Documented commitment to active-use reporting. Even where Microsoft can't agree to true-down, a documented commitment to provide active-use data on a quarterly basis gives finance the data to make internal reallocation decisions.

Practitioner's Takeaway

Copilot adoption is a deployment governance problem, not a product problem.

  • The technology works. The deployments that don't work are the ones without persona mapping, prompt-pattern training, and a reclaim cadence.
  • Track weekly active use, not seat count. Seat count tells you what you bought; active use tells you what you got.
  • The contract levers exist — true-down rights, co-term, reporting commitments. Use them.
  • The first quarterly reclaim is usually 15-25% of seats. Don't be surprised by it.

The shorter version

If your Copilot weekly active rate is below 40% at month four, you don't have a Copilot problem. You have a deployment governance gap. Fix that, and the line item starts paying for itself within two quarters.
