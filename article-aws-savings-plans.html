Cloud & FinOps

Why your AWS Savings Plans are leaving 18% on the table — and the unit-economics fix.

Most enterprises buy commitment based on last quarter's spend. The math that actually optimises is forward-loaded against workload elasticity. A worked example from a banking client.

VP
Vinod Patel
Managing Partner, Proteam Advisory
· 30 January 2026 · 5 min read

The standard AWS Savings Plans purchasing motion is roughly the following: take last quarter's compute spend, calculate a baseline, commit 70% of that baseline at the one-year or three-year rate, and pocket the discount. Across the 23 enterprise FinOps engagements we've worked in the past 18 months, that approach has consistently left between 14% and 22% of available savings on the table.

The math that actually optimises is forward-loaded against workload elasticity, not retrospective against last quarter's run rate. This is what the better-performing FinOps teams do differently.

Why the retrospective method underperforms

The retrospective method has two structural weaknesses.

It commits against historical demand, not future capacity. Most enterprise compute estates grow year-over-year. A three-year commitment based on last quarter's spend will under-commit relative to the actual workload of years two and three, leaving the most expensive on-demand pricing in place for the marginal workload that hasn't been committed.

It doesn't differentiate workload elasticity. A baseline 24/7 production workload and a daytime-only batch processing workload have very different commitment profiles. Treating them as a single pool wastes commitment on the elastic workload's troughs and overpays for the steady workload's peaks.

The unit-economics fix

The pattern that consistently outperforms involves three substitutions.

Substitute forward demand for retrospective spend

Rather than basing the commitment on last quarter, base it on a 12-month forward demand model that reflects planned capacity additions, known migrations, and confirmed workload retirements. The commitment level should target the floor of forward demand — the spend level below which the workload will not fall — rather than the centre or median.

Substitute workload-segmented purchasing for pooled purchasing

Steady production workloads (24/7, predictable, growing slowly) get committed at three-year No Upfront. Variable production workloads (24/7, growing or shrinking with seasonality) get committed at one-year No Upfront. Elastic workloads (batch, scheduled, dev/test) stay on Spot or on-demand. The result is higher coverage of the steady tier and lower waste on the elastic tier.

Substitute Compute Savings Plans for EC2 Instance Savings Plans where flexibility matters

EC2 Instance Savings Plans offer a deeper discount but lock you into a specific instance family in a specific region. Compute Savings Plans cost 4-5 percentage points more in headline rate, but apply across instance families, regions, OS types, and even Lambda. For workloads that haven't fully stabilised — especially anything in the early part of a Graviton migration or a multi-region failover plan — the flexibility is worth the headline rate difference.

The savings rate at which a Compute Savings Plan beats an Instance Savings Plan with 12% under-utilisation is around 4 percentage points. Most enterprises waste more than that on stranded Instance Savings Plans they negotiated three years ago.

A worked example: banking client, $14M annual EC2 spend

A retail banking client came to us with a three-year-old Reserved Instance + Savings Plan portfolio that had quietly drifted out of alignment with their actual workload. The diagnosis took eight working days. The remediation took six weeks.

Starting position

The client had committed at $9.6M of Reserved Instances + Savings Plans against an EC2 spend that had grown to $14M annually. Coverage rate looked acceptable on the surface (~68%) but masked two problems: the Reserved Instances were locked to instance families they were actively migrating away from (m5 to m7g), and the Savings Plans had been purchased at one-year rates when the workload had subsequently stabilised enough to support three-year commitments.

What we changed

  • Allowed the m5 RIs to expire on schedule rather than renewing. The Graviton migration was 70% complete; renewing would have stranded another $1.2M of commitment.
  • Replaced with three-year Compute Savings Plans (No Upfront) sized to the post-migration baseline. The flexibility across instance families covered the remaining migration work.
  • Restructured the Savings Plan portfolio into three tiers matched to workload elasticity: 24/7 production at three-year rates, variable production at one-year rates, dev/test on Spot.
  • Negotiated a private pricing discount on the EDP renewal in parallel, contingent on the increased commitment. The bundling produced an additional 6% on the published Savings Plan rates.

Twelve months later

Effective compute discount rate moved from 22% to 41%. Annual run-rate saving versus the prior portfolio: $2.7M, against a baseline that had grown 9% in the period. Coverage rate held at 76%, with the residual 24% intentionally on Spot or on-demand for elasticity.

Sitting on aging RIs while migrating to Graviton or switching regions? Run the math before you renew.

What to ask before the next renewal

Three questions, in order.

What is our forward 12-month demand floor — the spend level the workload won't fall below, given known retirements and migrations? Commit to that, not to last quarter's spend.

What proportion of our workload is genuinely steady versus seasonal versus elastic? Match commitment term to elasticity, not to a single pooled figure.

What is our migration velocity on instance families and regions? If you're more than 12 months from architectural stability, the headline rate difference between Compute and Instance Savings Plans is usually worth the flexibility.

Practitioner's Takeaway

Commitment optimisation is a forecasting problem, not a procurement problem.

  • Base commitments on forward demand floor, not last quarter's run rate.
  • Segment workloads by elasticity and match commitment term accordingly.
  • Compute Savings Plans usually beat Instance Savings Plans for any workload still under architectural change.
  • Bundle Savings Plan upgrades with EDP renewals — the cross-leverage is real.

The shorter version

The 18% leakage isn't because Savings Plans are mispriced. It's because most enterprises buy them looking backwards. Look forward, segment by elasticity, and the savings rate moves into the high-thirties without any heroic engineering.
